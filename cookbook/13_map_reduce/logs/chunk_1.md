Matching user requests to available nodes, maintaining quality standards, handling node failures, and establishing economic incentives for participation all require infrastructure that does not exist in any standardized form. Traditional service mesh approaches (Kubernetes, Istio) assume homogeneous, controlled infrastructure. They are not designed for nodes that join and leave dynamically, have heterogeneous capabilities, operate behind consumer NAT, or are owned by parties with no contractual relationship to the service operator. What is needed is a coordination protocol specifically designed for AI inference over heterogeneous, voluntary-participation infrastructure — analogous to how BitTorrent solved coordination for file distribution, or how SMTP solved coordination for email delivery. The Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) of a modern hyperscale data center — the ratio of total facility energy to IT equipment energy — ranges from 1.1 to 1.5. This means 10–50% of energy consumed by a data center is used for cooling, power conversion, and facility overhead, not computation. The square-cube law provides the physical intuition: as a data center grows, its volume (which generates heat) scales as r³ while its surface area (which dissipates heat) scales as r². Beyond a certain scale, passive cooling becomes physically impossible and active cooling systems — chillers, cooling towers, liquid cooling loops — become a major operational cost and energy consumer. Distributed nodes face no such constraint. A GPU running in a home office dissipates heat to the surrounding room, which in temperate climates requires no net energy for cooling (and in winter actually reduces heating demand). The marginal energy cost of adding inference capacity via distributed nodes is the inference computation itself — no cooling overhead, no power conversion losses beyond the device's own supply unit. 3.2 Idle Device Energy A critical and often overlooked factor is idle power consumption. A smartphone charges overnight regardless of whether it performs useful inference. A desktop workstation left on while its owner sleeps draws 50–150W whether or not its GPU is utilized. In both cases, the marginal energy cost of inference is the delta between idle and active power — not the full active power draw. Studies of always-on consumer devices suggest that the total idle energy consumed annually by the global installed base of compute-capable devices exceeds the total active energy consumed by all data centers. Distributed inference can convert a fraction of this already-consumed idle energy into useful computation with no net increase in global power demand. 3.3 Carbon Intensity and Geographic Distribution Data center operators typically co-locate with cheap energy sources, which in the United States and Europe increasingly means renewable sources. However, the correlation between cheap energy and renewable energy is imperfect, and marginal capacity additions — required to serve peak demand — frequently rely on dispatchable fossil sources. Distributed inference nodes are geographically distributed by nature. Peer-to-peer file sharing networks addressed this through tit-for-tat mechanisms (BitTorrent) or resource pooling (Tor). AI inference networks face a more complex incentive design problem because contributions and consumptions are not symmetric — inference requests have widely varying computational costs, and the value of a response to the requester bears no simple relationship to the cost of generating it. Token-based reward mechanisms — where node operators earn credits proportional to inference work performed — are the most widely proposed solution. Credits can be redeemed for inference capacity, converted to fiat currency, or donated. The economic model closely resembles frequent-flier miles or cloud compute credits, both of which have proven viable at scale. 4.2 Enterprise Adoption Dynamics For enterprise adopters, the value proposition of a distributed inference network is not primarily cost reduction — it is resilience and overflow capacity.
