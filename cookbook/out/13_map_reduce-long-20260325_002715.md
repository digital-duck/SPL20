Frontier model pricing, while declining, remains economically prohibitive for a substantial fraction of potential use cases. SECTION 2: DISTRIBUTED INFERENCE ARCHITECTURES

2.1 Peer-to-Peer Model Serving

Early experiments in peer-to-peer LLM serving (BitTorrent-inspired approaches, Petals from BigScience) demonstrated proof-of-concept viability but exposed a fundamental tension: transformer inference is not trivially parallelizable across heterogeneous, high-latency links. The attention mechanism requires synchronous communication between layers, and a single slow node in the pipeline degrades throughput for all participants.

More recent approaches partition the problem differently. Rather than pipelining a single large model across nodes, they route independent inference requests to separate nodes, each running a complete copy of a smaller model. This request-level parallelism avoids the synchronization problem entirely and degrades gracefully when nodes leave the network — requests are simply rerouted.

2.2 Speculative Decoding and Model Distillation

The gap between small and large models is narrowing faster than most analysts predicted. Speculative decoding — using a small "draft" model to generate candidate tokens that a larger "verifier" model accepts or rejects — effectively multiplies the throughput of large models by 2–4×. Meanwhile, knowledge distillation techniques have produced 7B-parameter models that match GPT-3.5 on a wide range of benchmarks, enabling inference on hardware as modest as a 2020-era laptop.

These developments shift the compute floor for useful inference downward. A GTX 1080 Ti (8GB VRAM, manufactured in 2017) can serve a quantized 7B model at 15–25 tokens/second — sufficient for most non-interactive batch tasks. The installed base of devices capable of this level of inference is measured in the hundreds of millions.

2.3 The Coordination Layer Problem

The missing piece in most distributed inference proposals is not the compute — it is the coordination. Matching user requests to available nodes, maintaining quality standards, handling node failures, and establishing economic incentives for participation all require infrastructure that does not exist in any standardized form.

Traditional service mesh approaches (Kubernetes, Istio) assume homogeneous, controlled infrastructure. They are not designed for nodes that join and leave dynamically, have heterogeneous capabilities, operate behind consumer NAT, or are owned by parties with no contractual relationship to the service operator.

What is needed is a coordination protocol specifically designed for AI inference over heterogeneous, voluntary-participation infrastructure — analogous to how BitTorrent solved coordination for file distribution, or how SMTP solved coordination for email delivery. The protocol needs to be open, so that participation is not gated by any single company; lightweight, so it runs on consumer devices; and economically rational, providing clear incentives for node operators.

SECTION 3: ENERGY AND ENVIRONMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS

3.1 The Thermodynamic Case for Distribution

Data centers are thermodynamically inefficient at scale. The Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) of a modern hyperscale data center — the ratio of total facility energy to IT equipment energy — ranges from 1.1 to 1.5. This means 10–50% of energy consumed by a data center is used for cooling, power conversion, and facility overhead, not computation.

The square-cube law provides the physical intuition: as a data center grows, its volume (which generates heat) scales as r³ while its surface area (which dissipates heat) scales as r². Beyond a certain scale, passive cooling becomes physically impossible and active cooling systems — chillers, cooling towers, liquid cooling loops — become a major operational cost and energy consumer.

Distributed nodes face no such constraint. A GPU running in a home office dissipates heat to the surrounding room, which in temperate climates requires no net energy for cooling (and in winter actually reduces heating demand). The marginal energy cost of adding inference capacity via distributed nodes is the inference computation itself — no cooling overhead, no power conversion losses beyond the device's own supply unit.

3.2 Idle Device Energy

A critical and often overlooked factor is idle power consumption. A smartphone charges overnight regardless of whether it performs useful inference. A desktop workstation left on while its owner sleeps draws 50–150W whether or not its GPU is utilized. In both cases, the marginal energy cost of inference is the delta between idle and active power — not the full active power draw.

Studies of always-on consumer devices suggest that the total idle energy consumed annually by the global installed base of compute-capable devices exceeds the total active energy consumed by all data centers. Distributed inference can convert a fraction of this already-consumed idle energy into useful computation with no net increase in global power demand.

3.3 Carbon Intensity and Geographic Distribution

Data center operators typically co-locate with cheap energy sources, which in the United States and Europe increasingly means renewable sources. However, the correlation between cheap energy and renewable energy is imperfect, and marginal capacity additions — required to serve peak demand — frequently rely on dispatchable fossil sources.

Distributed inference nodes are geographically distributed by nature. Across a sufficiently large network, the carbon intensity of the collective compute pool tends toward the average grid carbon intensity of the participating regions — which, as renewable penetration increases, declines over time without requiring any infrastructure investment by the network operator.

SECTION 4: ECONOMIC MODELS FOR DISTRIBUTED INFERENCE

4.1 The Incentive Design Problem

Distributed systems that require voluntary participation are subject to the tragedy of the commons: each individual participant has an incentive to consume resources without contributing. Peer-to-peer file sharing networks addressed this through tit-for-tat mechanisms (BitTorrent) or resource pooling (Tor). AI inference networks face a more complex incentive design problem because contributions and consumptions are not symmetric — inference requests have widely varying computational costs, and the value of a response to the requester bears no simple relationship to the cost of generating it.

Token-based reward mechanisms — where node operators earn credits proportional to inference work performed — are the most widely proposed solution. Credits can be redeemed for inference capacity, converted to fiat currency, or donated. The economic model closely resembles frequent-flier miles or cloud compute credits, both of which have proven viable at scale.

4.2 Enterprise Adoption Dynamics

For enterprise adopters, the value proposition of a distributed inference network is not primarily cost reduction — it is resilience and overflow capacity. A financial institution running AI-assisted document review cannot accept a dependency on a single cloud provider. A distributed inference grid, accessed through a standard API, provides the same geographic and provider diversity that CDNs provide for content delivery.

The enterprise adoption curve is likely to follow the CDN analogy: early adopters are organizations with strict availability requirements or data residency constraints, followed by organizations seeking cost optimization for high-volume, non-latency-sensitive workloads, followed by mainstream adoption as the network effects of a large contributing node pool reduce latency to competitive levels.

4.3 The Network Effect Flywheel

Distributed inference networks exhibit classic two-sided network effects. More contributing nodes improve availability, reduce latency, and lower per-token costs — attracting more developers and users. More users generate more inference revenue, which increases rewards for node operators — attracting more contributing nodes. The challenge is achieving sufficient critical mass on both sides simultaneously.

Historical precedents suggest that open protocols combined with a compelling initial use case can bootstrap this flywheel. SMTP was adopted first by universities sharing research; BitTorrent was adopted first by open-source software distributors. The initial use case for distributed AI inference is likely to be non-latency-sensitive batch workloads: document summarization, content classification, data extraction — tasks where a 10-second response time is acceptable and cost and privacy matter more than speed.

SECTION 5: OPEN STANDARDS AND INTEROPERABILITY

5.1 The Case for an Open Protocol

Proprietary distributed inference solutions — where a single company controls both the coordination protocol and the node software — reproduce the vendor lock-in problems they purport to solve. An enterprise that routes its inference workloads through a proprietary distributed network is no less dependent on that vendor than one using a centralized cloud API.

An open protocol, analogous to SMTP for email or HTTP for the web, would allow any node operator to participate, any developer to build clients, and any organization to run its own coordination infrastructure. The protocol would define the wire format for inference requests and responses, the capability advertisement mechanism for nodes, and the accounting primitives for tracking and rewarding contributions.

5.2 Existing Standards Gaps

No such standard currently exists. The OpenAI API format has become a de facto standard for the request/response interface, but it does not address node discovery, capability matching, load balancing, or economic accounting. The MCP (Model Context Protocol) from Anthropic addresses tool integration but not distributed execution. W3C and IETF have not yet engaged with AI inference interoperability as a standardization problem.

The gap is an opportunity. The organization that publishes a well-designed open protocol for distributed AI inference — with reference implementations, clear governance, and Apache 2.0 licensing — stands to become the foundational infrastructure layer for the next generation of AI deployment, in the same way that the Linux Foundation or Apache Software Foundation became foundational for cloud infrastructure.

SECTION 6: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The centralization of AI inference in a small number of hyperscale data centers is a temporary equilibrium driven by the current scarcity of specialized hardware and the absence of a viable coordination protocol for distributed inference. Both conditions are changing.

Hardware scarcity will ease as semiconductor manufacturing capacity for advanced GPUs expands, as model distillation produces capable models that run on commodity hardware, and as speculative decoding multiplies the effective throughput of existing infrastructure. The installed base of devices capable of running useful inference is already large and growing.

The coordination problem is solvable with current technology. The required components — request routing, node capability advertisement, quality assurance, and economic accounting — are individually understood engineering problems. What is missing is not technical capability but organizational will to build and govern an open standard.

The organizations best positioned to do this are not the hyperscale cloud providers, which have structural incentives to maintain centralization, but independent open-source projects, universities, and startups whose economic interests align with openness. The window for establishing an open standard before proprietary solutions achieve sufficient network effects is probably three to five years.

For enterprises evaluating AI infrastructure strategy, the recommendation is to design systems that are provider-agnostic from the first line of code: use standard interfaces, avoid proprietary SDK dependencies, and plan for a hybrid architecture that can route workloads between centralized and distributed execution depending on task requirements, cost, and availability.

-- Recipe 13: Map-Reduce Summarizer
-- Splits a large document into chunks, summarizes each chunk (map),
-- then combines all summaries into a final summary (reduce).
-- Demonstrates the map-reduce pattern for handling content beyond context limits.
--
-- Usage:
--   spl run cookbook/13_map_reduce/map_reduce.spl --tools cookbook/13_map_reduce/tools.py --adapter ollama -m gemma3 document="$(cat large_doc.txt)"
--   spl run cookbook/13_map_reduce/map_reduce.spl --tools cookbook/13_map_reduce/tools.py --adapter ollama document="$(cat README.md)" style="bullet points"
--   spl run cookbook/13_map_reduce/map_reduce.spl --tools cookbook/13_map_reduce/tools.py --adapter ollama document="long text here..." style="executive brief"
--
-- Tools: chunk_plan(), extract_chunk() are in tools.py (CALL, zero tokens).
--        write_file(), read_file() are SPL built-ins — no --tools flag needed.
--        summarize_chunk(), reduce_summaries(), quality_score(), improve_summary() call the LLM.
--
-- Logs written to @log_dir (default: cookbook/13_map_reduce/logs):
--   chunk_00.md, chunk_01.md, ...    — raw extracted chunks
--   summary_00.md, summary_01.md, ... — per-chunk LLM summaries
--   final_summary.md                  — combined final summary

WORKFLOW map_reduce_summarizer
    INPUT:  
        @document TEXT, 
        @style TEXT,
        @log_dir TEXT DEFAULT 'cookbook/13_map_reduce/logs'
    OUTPUT: @final_summary TEXT
DO
    @chunk_index := 0
    @summaries := []          -- LIST: accumulates per-chunk summaries

    LOGGING 'Starting map-reduce | document length: ' + @document

    -- Step 1: Determine how to split the document (deterministic, 0 tokens)
    CALL chunk_plan(@document) INTO @chunk_count

    LOGGING 'Document split into ' + @chunk_count + ' chunks'

    -- Step 2: MAP — summarize each chunk independently
    WHILE @chunk_index < @chunk_count DO
        CALL extract_chunk(@document, @chunk_index, @chunk_count) INTO @chunk   -- deterministic, 0 tokens

        -- Save raw chunk to logs/
        CALL write_file(@chunk, f'{@log_dir}/chunk_{@chunk_index}.md') INTO @_

        GENERATE summarize_chunk(@chunk, @chunk_index) INTO @chunk_summary

        -- Save chunk summary to logs/
        CALL write_file(@chunk_summary, f'{@log_dir}/summary_{@chunk_index}.md') INTO @_

        LOGGING f'[Chunk {@chunk_index}/{@chunk_count}] summary saved'

        @summaries := list_append(@summaries, @chunk_summary)
        @chunk_index := @chunk_index + 1
    END

    -- Step 3: REDUCE — concat all chunk summaries, then combine into final
    @summaries_text := list_concat(@summaries, '\n')
    GENERATE reduce_summaries(@summaries_text, @style) INTO @final_summary

    -- Step 4: Quality check the final summary
    GENERATE quality_score(@final_summary, @document) INTO @score

    EVALUATE @score
        WHEN > 0.7 THEN
            CALL write_file(@final_summary, f'{@log_dir}/final_summary.md') INTO @_
            LOGGING f'Final summary saved to {@log_dir}/final_summary.md (score={@score})'
            COMMIT @final_summary WITH status = 'complete', chunks = @chunk_count
        ELSE
            GENERATE improve_summary(@final_summary, @summaries_text) INTO @final_summary
            CALL write_file(@final_summary, f'{@log_dir}/final_summary.md') INTO @_
            LOGGING f'Improved summary saved to {@log_dir}/final_summary.md (score={@score})'
            COMMIT @final_summary WITH status = 'refined', chunks = @chunk_count
    END

EXCEPTION
    WHEN ContextLengthExceeded THEN
        @summaries_text := list_concat(@summaries, '\n')
        GENERATE reduce_summaries(@summaries_text, @style) INTO @final_summary
        CALL write_file(@final_summary, f'{@log_dir}/final_summary.md') INTO @_
        COMMIT @final_summary WITH status = 'partial'
    WHEN BudgetExceeded THEN
        @summaries_text := list_concat(@summaries, '\n')
        GENERATE reduce_summaries(@summaries_text, @style) INTO @final_summary
        CALL write_file(@final_summary, f'{@log_dir}/final_summary.md') INTO @_
        COMMIT @final_summary WITH status = 'budget_limit'
END

Distributed inference can convert a fraction of this already-consumed idle energy into useful computation with no net increase in global power demand.

3.3 Carbon Intensity and Geographic Distribution

Data center operators typically co-locate with cheap energy sources, which in the United States and Europe increasingly means renewable sources. However, the correlation between cheap energy and renewable energy is imperfect, and marginal capacity additions — required to serve peak demand — frequently rely on dispatchable fossil sources.

Distributed inference nodes are geographically distributed by nature. Across a sufficiently large network, the carbon intensity of the collective compute pool tends toward the average grid carbon intensity of the participating regions — which, as renewable penetration increases, declines over time without requiring any infrastructure investment by the network operator.

SECTION 4: ECONOMIC MODELS FOR DISTRIBUTED INFERENCE

4.1 The Incentive Design Problem

Distributed systems that require voluntary participation are subject to the tragedy of the commons: each individual participant has an incentive to consume resources without contributing. Peer-to-peer file sharing networks addressed this through tit-for-tat mechanisms (BitTorrent) or resource pooling (Tor). AI inference networks face a more complex incentive design problem because contributions and consumptions are not symmetric — inference requests have widely varying computational costs, and the value of a response to the requester bears no simple relationship to the cost of generating it.

Token-based reward mechanisms — where node operators earn credits proportional to inference work performed — are the most widely proposed solution. Credits can be redeemed for inference capacity, converted to fiat currency, or donated. The economic model closely resembles frequent-flier miles or cloud compute credits, both of which have proven viable at scale.

4.2 Enterprise Adoption Dynamics

For enterprise adopters, the value proposition of a distributed inference network is not primarily cost reduction — it is resilience and overflow capacity. A financial institution running AI-assisted document review cannot accept a dependency on a single cloud provider. A distributed inference grid, accessed through a standard API, provides the same geographic and provider diversity that CDNs provide for content delivery.

The enterprise adoption curve is likely to follow the CDN analogy: early adopters are organizations with strict availability requirements or data residency constraints, followed by organizations seeking cost optimization for high-volume, non-latency-sensitive workloads, followed by mainstream adoption as the network effects of a large contributing node pool reduce latency to competitive levels.

4.3 The Network Effect Flywheel

Distributed inference networks exhibit classic two-sided network effects. More contributing nodes improve availability, reduce latency, and lower per-token costs — attracting more developers and users. More users generate more inference revenue, which increases rewards for node operators — attracting more contributing nodes. The challenge is achieving sufficient critical mass on both sides simultaneously.

Historical precedents suggest that open protocols combined with a compelling initial use case can bootstrap this flywheel. SMTP was adopted first by universities sharing research; BitTorrent was adopted first by open-source software distributors. The initial use case for distributed AI inference is likely to be non-latency-sensitive batch workloads: document summarization, content classification, data extraction — tasks where a 10-second response time is acceptable and cost and privacy matter more than speed.

SECTION 5: OPEN STANDARDS AND INTEROPERABILITY

5.1 The Case for an Open Protocol

Proprietary distributed inference solutions — where a single company controls both the coordination protocol and the node software — reproduce the vendor lock-in problems they purport to solve. An enterprise that routes its inference workloads through a proprietary distributed network is no less dependent on that vendor than one using a centralized cloud API.

An open protocol, analogous to SMTP for email or HTTP for the web, would allow any node operator to participate, any developer to build clients, and any organization to run its own coordination infrastructure. The protocol would define the wire format for inference requests and responses, the capability advertisement mechanism for nodes, and the accounting primitives for tracking and rewarding contributions.

5.2 Existing Standards Gaps

No such standard currently exists. The OpenAI API format has become a de facto standard for the request/response interface, but it does not address node discovery, capability matching, load balancing, or economic accounting. The MCP (Model Context Protocol) from Anthropic addresses tool integration but not distributed execution. W3C and IETF have not yet engaged with AI inference interoperability as a standardization problem.

The gap is an opportunity. The organization that publishes a well-designed open protocol for distributed AI inference — with reference implementations, clear governance, and Apache 2.0 licensing — stands to become the foundational infrastructure layer for the next generation of AI deployment, in the same way that the Linux Foundation or Apache Software Foundation became foundational for cloud infrastructure.

SECTION 6: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The centralization of AI inference in a small number of hyperscale data centers is a temporary equilibrium driven by the current scarcity of specialized hardware and the absence of a viable coordination protocol for distributed inference. Both conditions are changing.

Hardware scarcity will ease as semiconductor manufacturing capacity for advanced GPUs expands, as model distillation produces capable models that run on commodity hardware, and as speculative decoding multiplies the effective throughput of existing infrastructure. The installed base of devices capable of running useful inference is already large and growing.

The coordination problem is solvable with current technology. The required components — request routing, node capability advertisement, quality assurance, and economic accounting — are individually understood engineering problems. What is missing is not technical capability but organizational will to build and govern an open standard.

The organizations best positioned to do this are not the hyperscale cloud providers, which have structural incentives to maintain centralization, but independent open-source projects, universities, and startups whose economic interests align with openness. The window for establishing an open standard before proprietary solutions achieve sufficient network effects is probably three to five years.

For enterprises evaluating AI infrastructure strategy, the recommendation is to design systems that are provider-agnostic from the first line of code: use standard interfaces, avoid proprietary SDK dependencies, and plan for a hybrid architecture that can route workloads between centralized and distributed execution depending on task requirements, cost, and availability.
