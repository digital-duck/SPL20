**Refined Explanation:**

Inflation occurs when the general price level of goods and services in an economy rises over time. It is typically caused by a combination of factors that increase demand for goods and services or raise the costs of producing them. These causes can be categorized into four primary types:

1.  **Demand-Pull Inflation:** This happens when aggregate demand (the total demand for all goods and services) increases faster than the economy's ability to supply them at current price levels.
    *   It is often driven by factors such as:
        *   **Increased Consumer Spending:** Can result from higher disposable income (e.g., tax cuts, wage hikes), increased household wealth (e.g., asset gains), or rising consumer confidence and credit availability. Government spending increases can also inject money into the economy.
        *   **Increased Business Investment:** Companies borrowing excessively to fund expansion, new factories, or equipment can amplify overall demand.

2.  **Cost-Push Inflation:** This arises when the cost of producing goods and services (aggregate supply) increases significantly due to higher input costs or reduced production capacity, forcing businesses to raise prices.
    *   Key drivers include:
        *   **Higher Raw Material Costs:** Increases in commodities like oil, metals, or agricultural products directly boost production expenses. Supply chain disruptions from natural disasters, pandemics, or geopolitical issues can also force price increases.

3.  **Monetary Inflation:** This is caused by an increase in the money supply relative to the level of goods and services.
    *   Central bank actions are a primary cause:
        *   **Excessive Growth of Money Supply:** Can occur through traditional currency printing or more modern methods like quantitative easing (purchasing assets to inject liquidity) or maintaining persistently low interest rates, which encourage borrowing and spending.

4.  **Built-in Inflation (Expectations):** This is an inflationary expectation that becomes self-fulfilling.
    *   It stems from:
        *   Workers demanding wage increases to keep up with rising prices they anticipate.
        *   Businesses preemptively raising prices because they expect higher costs or wages in the future, reinforcing the cycle.

It's crucial to understand that these causes are interconnected and often influence each other. For instance, a rise in oil prices (a Cost-Push factor) can increase transportation costs, contributing to Demand-Pull inflation if it stimulates further spending due to higher energy bills. Similarly, Monetary Inflation can feed into all three types over time.
