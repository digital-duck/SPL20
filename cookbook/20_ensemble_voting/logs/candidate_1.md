Inflation is caused by various factors that can be broadly categorized into supply-side and demand-side influences. Here’s a detailed explanation of these factors:

### Demand-Pull Inflation
1. **Increase in Aggregate Demand**: When the overall level of spending (demand) in an economy increases more rapidly than the production capacity, it leads to higher prices. This can happen due to government stimulus measures, expansionary monetary policies by central banks, or increased consumer and business confidence leading to greater expenditure.
2. **Budget Deficits and Fiscal Policy**: Large budget deficits funded through borrowing increase the money supply in an economy, potentially leading to inflation if the increase in demand outpaces production capacity.

### Cost-Push Inflation
1. **Increases in Input Costs**: If the prices of raw materials or labor rise significantly, businesses may pass these increased costs on to consumers in the form of higher prices.
2. **Wage-Price Spiral**: This occurs when workers demand higher wages due to rising living costs (e.g., food and housing), which companies then try to offset by raising product prices.

### Built-In Inflation Expectations
1. **Expectations of Future Inflation**: If people expect inflation to continue, they might demand higher wages now, anticipating that their salaries will be eroded by future price increases. This anticipation can become a self-fulfilling prophecy as businesses respond by raising prices.

### Supply-Side Factors
1. **Productivity Growth and Technological Change**: Slow productivity growth or technological change can limit production capacity, making it harder to meet demand without raising prices.
2. **Natural Disasters and Other Shocks**: Events such as natural disasters, wars, or pandemics disrupt supply chains and reduce the supply of goods and services, pushing up prices.

### Structural Factors
1. **Global Commodity Prices**: The rise in global commodity prices (like oil, metals, food) can feed into domestic inflation if these commodities are an essential input for production.
2. **Monetary Policy**: Inflation is also influenced by the central bank's monetary policy decisions. Low interest rates and easy money supply policies can stimulate spending and investment but may lead to higher inflation if not managed properly.

### International Trade
1. **Exchange Rates and Import Prices**: If a country’s currency depreciates, imported goods become more expensive, which can increase overall prices in the domestic market.
2. **Terms of Trade**: An unfavorable shift in a nation's terms of trade (the ratio of export to import prices) can lead to inflation as it reduces the purchasing power of exports.

### Policy and Institutional Factors
1. **Government Policies**: Certain government policies, like subsidies or protectionism, can distort market conditions, leading to inefficiencies that might result in higher prices.
2. **Institutional Corruption and Market Failures**: Poor institutional governance and regulatory failures can also contribute to inflation through corruption, rent-seeking behavior, and lack of effective competition.

### Summary
Inflation is a complex phenomenon driven by multiple factors operating at different levels—individual, corporate, national, and global. Understanding the specific causes requires analyzing each country's unique economic context and market dynamics.
